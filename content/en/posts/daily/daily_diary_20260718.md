*Written July 18, 2026, 20:37*

Lately, spending my weekends developing with AI has become a habit, and it keeps stirring up thoughts like this:

*'Ah, at this rate my job is going to be replaced easily.'*

Whether it's CLAUDE or CODEX, AI keeps advancing day by day, and when you tell it to code, it finishes writing a page with ease.

What they produce is better in quality than I'd expect, and they grasp the context from just a little explanation. When they carefully consider what I intended and figure out which direction I'm heading, it even gives me chills.

*'This thing isn't human, is it?'*

Of course, there are problems too. AI is deeply learned. Almost omniscient, literally. But it's also foolish.

Once, I told Codex to review a harness, and I caught it making a fuss: 'The guardrail is blocking Bash commands too simply. If it were me, I'd block ways to bypass it by concatenating strings, or by encoding in Base64.'

But in reality, the one running bash inside it is the AI, and I'd already set writing and deleting to require permission or to be allowed only within a fixed scope — so blocking "bypasses" makes no sense, and there are infinitely many ways to bypass it in the first place. The thing was racing toward an answer it could never reach, evaporating my tokens in the process. Just like the flight of Icarus.

---

At times like this, a thought comes to me: won't the center of gravity of the work shift from development to planning and supervision?

For a developer, development isn't actually the whole job. Planning, communication, maintenance, optimization, and so on... there's a reason a developer's work is divided into so many areas.

But now, with the arrival of AI, development work as a whole is changing so that it can be done without human hands. So then?

Won't planning ability, rather than interpreting source code, come first for a developer now?

A developer at a certain big Silicon Valley company — whom I recently subscribed to and whose videos I've been watching steadily — once showed how they work: they had left development almost entirely to AI, and the developer's main job had become analyzing plans and conversing with the AI.

Of course, given the nature of the domestic market, that kind of change will take more time here, but someday it will shift in that direction — that's what I think.

If only for that day, isn't building even a little rapport with this learned fool my most urgent task right now?

Today, once again, I tell the AI:

*'Hey AI, this is important, so don't forget it, okay?'*

Please.
